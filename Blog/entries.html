Example of Bootstrap and Schauder estimates

Posted by: Daniel B.
[2022-11-23 Wed]

In the variational approach, harmonic functions can be understood as local minimisers of the Dirichlet energy functional. Then \(u\) is harmonic in a domain \(\Omega\) if it is a minimiser of the functional

\begin{equation} E(v) = \int_{\Omega} |\nabla v|^2 dx. \end{equation}

In a more general case we may want to understand minimiser \(u\) of the integral

\begin{equation} E(u) = \int_{\Omega} \mathcal{F}(\nabla u) dx, \end{equation}

where \(\mathcal{F}:\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}\). Then, the Euler-Lagrange equation for the minimiser takes the form

\begin{equation} D_{i} F^{i}(\nabla u) = 0. \end{equation}

Here one should read

\begin{equation} D_{i} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x_{i}}, \end{equation}

and for \(p = (p_1, \ldots, p_{n})\in \mathbb{R}^n\),

\begin{equation} F^{i}(\nabla u) = \left.\frac{\partial \mathcal{F}}{\partial p_{i}}\right|_{p = \nabla u}. \end{equation}

Then we can write

\begin{equation} D_{i} F^{i}(\nabla u) = F^{i,j}(\nabla u) D_{ij}u = b^{ij}D_{ij}u = 0. \end{equation}

\(\mathcal{F}\) may be assumed to be smooth and, in order to put this into the frame of linear elliptic equations, strictly convex, i.e. \(F^{i,j}\) (and hence \(b^{ij}\)) to be positive definite. In principle it is possible to assume that \(\nabla u \in L^2\).

If we could show that \(\nabla u\) is Hölder continuous (or equivalently that \(u\) is \(C^{1,\alpha}\)) then the elliptic regularity and the Schauder estimates for linear equations would imply that \(u\) is \(C^{\infty}\).

The bootstrap argument: If \(\nabla u \in C^{\alpha}\), then the coefficients of the equation

\begin{equation} b^{ij}D_{ij}u = 0 \end{equation}

are in \(C^{\alpha}\). In the standard theory of linear elliptic equations the Schauder estimates imply that the solution \(u\) is in \(C^{2,\alpha}\). But then, this improves the regularity of the gradients, i.e., \(\nabla u \in C^{1,\alpha}\). It is possible to differentiate the equation and see that any directional derivative \(D_{v}u\) satisfy another linear equation with \(C^{\alpha}\) coefficients, which in turns shows that \(\nabla u \in C^{2,\alpha}\), this is \(u\in C^{3,\alpha}\). Then by differentiating the equation…. \(u\) is smooth.

The passage from \(\nabla u \in L^2\) to \(\nabla u \in C^{\alpha}\) is due to the outstanding development of the now call De Georgi-Nash-Moser theorem for equations in divergence form.

Existence if Isothermal Coordinates.

Posted by: Daniel B.
[2022-09-26 Mon]

In Riemannian geometry, the isothermal coordinates are local coordinates on \((M,g)\) in which the expression of the metric is conformal to the Euclidean one, that is, the Riemannian metric \(g\) can be expressed locally as

\begin{equation} g = f(x) \left( dx_1^2 + \cdots + dx_2^2\right), \end{equation}

where \(f:M\to \mathbb{R}\) is a smooth positive function.

In the two dimensional case, Dennis M. Deturck and Jerry L. Kazdan describe the method of obtaining these isothermal coordinates. Starting with one harmonic function \(u\) such that \(\nabla u (p) \neq\) at some \(p\in M\), then the second coordinate function \(v\) is given by the harmonic conjugate of \(u\), and this can be obtained by solving

\begin{equation} dv = \star du. \end{equation}

Note that if \(u\) is harmonic, since the one form \(\star du\) is a closed form, the existence of \(v\) follows. Here the Hodge star \(\star\) maps one-forms to one-forms.

The function \(v\) is harmonic because of the identities

\begin{equation} \label{eq:dsd} \Delta v = \star d \star d v = - \star dd u = 0. \end{equation}

What happens in higher dimensions? In any dimension it is possible to find harmonic coordinates, such that \(g_{ij}(p) = \delta_{ij}\) in composition with a linear transformation, nevertheless, there is no satisfactory generalisation of harmonic conjugate.

We want to unveil the equation \eqref{eq:dsd} and the existence theory form PDE's that is used here.

In the following updates, I will be discussing among other the following results and how they will help to prove the existence of isothmeral coordinates.

If the differential 1-form \(\star du\) is closed, then the function \(u\) is harmonic.

Poincaré's lemma: On a contractible manifold, all closed forms are exact.

Assume that \(F:M \to N\) is \(C^{\infty}\) that \(m\in M\), and that \(dF_m: T_{m}M \to T_{F(m)}N\) is an isomorphism. Then there is a neighborhood \(U\) of \(m\) such that \(F: U \to F(U)\) is a diffeomorphism onto the open set \(F(U)\) in N.

A set of smooth functions \(y_1, y_2, \ldots, y_k\) defined on some neighbourhood of \(m\in M\) it is called an independent set at the point \(m\) if the differentials \(dy_1, dy_2, \ldots, dy_k\) form an independnt set in \(T_{m}M^*\).

Example of a non-integrable plane field in \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

Posted by: Daniel B.
[2022-02-23 Wed]

The first example in the book of V.I. Arnold, Lectures on Partial Differential Equations is that of a plane field in the Euclidean three dimensional space that has no integral surface, hence, integrable fields of planes are an exceptional phenomenon.

In the Euclidean space with coordinates \((x,y,z)\), we consider the field of planes given by the equation \(dz = ydx\). Prove that no surface whose tangent plane at every point coincides with the plane of the field.

The proof is by contradiction. After drawing the plane field one realises that these will never have a normal direction which is parallel to the \(xy -\) plane. Then if such a surface \(S\) exists, it can be locally parameterised as the graph of a \(C^2\) function \(u: U\subset \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}\) around a point \(p \in U \subset S\), i.e., for every \((x,y)\in U\), we have \((x,y,u(x,y))\in S\).

Now, since we have \(dz = y \, dx\), it must hold that

\begin{equation} du = u_x dx + u_y dy = y \, dx. \end{equation}

This last identity implies that \(u_x = y\). Thus \(u_{xy} = 1\) which is impossible since on the other hand we also have \(u_y = 0\).

In the case of an Ordinary Differential equation, the geometric situation is easier, since for every smooth vector field \(F\) in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) and a point \(p\in \mathbb{R}^3\), there exists a smooth integral curve of \(F\) through \(p\).

For general dimensions, an integrable field of hypersurfaces is that for which there exists a submanifold whose tangent spaces at each point is a proper subspace of the given hypersurfaces.

The Geometry of the support function

Posted by: Daniel B.
[2020-08-31 Mon]

One of the most useful tools I have learnt when dealing with convex geometry is that of the support function. It has been used to prove the uniformly ellipticity of a given geometric fully nonlinear elliptic equation, or to get estimates on the principal curvatures of a strictly convex hypersurface.

Consider the Euclidean space \(R^n\) with its inner product \(\cdot\). Then, any hyperplane can be defined by a point in this plane \(x_0\) and a unit normal vector \(\nu \in R^n\) as all \(x\in R^n\) satisfying the equation

\begin{equation} \nu \cdot (x_0 - x) = 0. \end{equation}

One can also think of \(\nu = \nu(x)\) as the constant unit vector field along the hyperplane, and then the last equation says that the function \(\varphi(x) = \nu(x)\cdot x\) has a constant value, namely, the distance from the origin to the hyperplane.

If \(\Sigma \subset R^{n+1}\) is a strictly convex hypersurface in the Euclidean space, then the Gauss map \(n:\Sigma\to S^n\) is a diffeomorphism with the unit sphere \(S^n\). At every \(x\in\Sigma\), corresponds one unit vector \(n(x)\) which is perpendicular to the tangent hyperplane to \(\Sigma\) at \(x\). Then one can define the support function of \(\Sigma\), \(u:S^n \to R\) as

\begin{equation} u(\xi) = \xi \cdot n^{-1}(\xi), \quad \xi \in S^n \subset R^{n+1}. \end{equation}

Note that the tangent hyperplane to \(\Sigma\) at \(n^{-1}(\xi)\) has a unit normal vector \(\xi\) then the support function is clearly the distance from the origin of \(R^{n+1}\) to this hyperplane.

Brownian Motion and Harmonic Functions

Posted by: Daniel B.
[2019-10-23 Wed]

This is one of the relations that I always forget, so I will keep it here for the future. When I hear someone speaking about Brownian motion immediately comes to my head a voice saying … you know, the probabilistic representation of harmonic functions… but never manage to remember how this link is done.

Here are the ingredients I need to build the bridge:

  • A domain in \(\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^n\).
  • A Brownian motion starting at \(x\in \Omega\), i.e. \(X^*_t = x + X_t\).
  • A pay off function \(\varphi:\partial\Omega \to \mathbb{R}\), so the first time the Brownian motion hits the boundary at \(z\in\partial\Omega\) we get a payoff \(\varphi(z)\).

If \(T\) is the first hitting time of \(X^*_T\), define

\begin{equation} u(x) = \mathbb{E}[\varphi(X^*_T)]. \end{equation}

Now consider the ball \(B_r(x)\) contained in \(\Omega\). The key observation is that from the properties of Brownian motion \(X^*_t\) its possible to conclude that the process will hit any point in the boundary with the same probability.

This observation implies that \(u\) satisfies the mean value property, and consequently is a solution of the following Dirichlet problem:

\begin{equation} \left\{ \begin{array}{cccc} \Delta u &=& 0& \mbox{in}\quad \Omega\\ u &=& \varphi & \mbox{on}\quad \partial\Omega\\ \end{array}\right. \end{equation}

Existence of Geodesics With a Heat Flow.

Posted by: Daniel B.
[2019-05-14 Tue]

If we want to obtain a closed geodesic in a compact Riemannian manifold all we have to do is get one loop and wait. Yes just wait long enough and, surprisingly, our loop will transform into a geodesic one. Moreover, take a smooth close loop in a compact Riemannian manifold, of course this belongs to a homotopy class. Suppose now that we are able to measure the energy of our loop with some sort of thermometer, and that changes of this energy may produce some elongation or contraction of the loop, measured by its length. Then we wait for a reasonable long period of time for our loop to cool down and.. we end up with a geodesic of the same homotopy class!

Well, unfortunately not quite. The problem of passing from \(t_n\) to \(t\) (see below) in the convergence is something that needs to be treated carefully.

The curve \(\gamma : (a,b) \subset \mathbb{R} \to M\) is geodesic if and only if in local coordinates it satisfies

\begin{equation} \ddot{\gamma}^k(t) + \dot{\gamma}^i(t)\dot{\gamma}^j)(t)\Gamma_{ij}^{k}(\gamma(t)) = 0. \end{equation}

Before we continue just a word of warning: note that for any compact Riemannian manifold, and two points in it, there are always more than one geodesic joining them, a quick example of this is the sphere with the North and South pole. The uniqueness of the shortest geodesic is guaranteed when the two points are sufficiently close. The method we will describe, produces a geodesic but It may not necessarily be the shortest one.

Theorem Let \(M\) be a compact Riemannian manifold. Then every homotopy class of closed curves in \(M\) contains a geodesic.

To apply the heat flow approach, we consider the following family of curves:

\begin{equation} u : S^1 \times [0,\infty) \to M, \end{equation}

where \(S^1\) is the unite circle, and the parabolic equation

\begin{equation} \begin{split} u_{t} &= u^k_{ss} + u^i_{s}u^j_{s}\Gamma_{ij}^k & \,\,s\in S^1, t\geq 0,\\ u(s,0)& = \gamma(s) & \,\, s\in S^1. \end{split} \end{equation}

The Heat Flow Method

Now we write the method to obtain the geodesic as outlined by J. Jost in his Riemannian Geometry and Geometric Analysis.

  1. Short time existence. There exist \(T_0>0\) such that the initial value problem above has a solution \(u^k_{t} = u^k_{ss} + u^i_{s}u^j_{s}\Gamma_{ij}^k\) in \(S^1\times [0,T_0)\) and \(u(s,0)=\gamma(s)\). Then the maximal (time) interval of existence of a solution is nonempty and open.
  2. A priori estimates of spacial derivatives. Let \(u(s,t)\) be a solution. Get an a priori estimate of the spacial derivative \(|u_s| \leq C\), where \(C\) independently of \(t\). Then we can think of the equation \(u_t - u_{ss} =f\), where \(f\) is a bounded function. Then regularity theory for linear parabolic equations is used to control higher order derivatives which are time independent.
  3. Convergence as \(t\) goes to \(\infty\). This a priori estimate implies the following: If a solution exists in \([0,T_{0})\), and for \(t\to T_{0}\) the solution \(u(s,t)\) will converge to a smooth curve \(u(s,T_{0})\). Then this curve can be taken as the new initial value and continue the solution for times bigger than \(T_{0}\). Then the maximal interval of existence of a solution is also a closed set. This at the end implies that a solution exists for all times \(t>0\).
  4. The Energy functional is decreasing. Note that the energy \(E(u(s,t))\) is decreasing in \(t\) and since \(0 \leq E(u(s,t))\) for all \(t\), it is possible to find a sequence \(\{t_n\}\) such that \(u(s,t) \to \tilde{\gamma}(s)\), \(t\to \infty\), and \(\tilde{\gamma}\) is such that \(\tilde{\gamma}^k_{ss} + \tilde{\gamma}^i_{s}\tilde{\gamma}^j_{s}\Gamma_{ij}^k = 0\).
  5. Convexity. If convexity is not fulfilled, then things that may look like wiggles may happen to have less energy than the geodesic. We also have to be careful with the curvature of our manifold. When (non-negative) curvature condition is fulfilled and convexity is checked, then the convergence to the geodesic can be taken from \(t\to\infty\), and is not restricted to the sequence \(t_n\).

One of the things I learnt from this exercise is to be careful when using normal coordinates and integrating by parts. We will go through the computations of \(E\), \(E_t\) and \(E_{tt}\).

The Energy functional of a curve \(\alpha\) is given by \(E(u):= \frac{1}{2}\int_{S^1} \|\dot{\alpha}(t)\|^2dt\), equivalently in coordinates with \(\alpha(s,t)\) and \(\alpha_s\) to denote \(\partial_s \alpha(s,t) = \dot{\alpha}(s,t)\) we write

\begin{equation} E(u(\cdot,t)) = \frac{1}{2}\int_{S^1} g_{ij}\alpha^i_s\alpha^j_s ds, \end{equation}

then

\begin{equation} \partial_t E = \frac{1}{2}\int_{S^1} \partial_t(g_{ij}) + 2g_{ij}\alpha^i_{st}\alpha^j_s ds, \end{equation}

Geometry of hypersurfaces.

Posted by: Daniel B.
[2019-05-16 Thu]

When we are trying to understand the geometry of a space, we usually wonder what are the properties whithin the space. Gauss's Theorema Egregium, for example, shows how for surfaces in the three dimensional space the Gaussian curvature is an intrinsic invariant. On the other hand, we have also properties that change depending on how one represents the geometric objects.

Let \((M^{n+1},g)\) be a Riemannian manifold and \(\Sigma^n\subset M^{n+1}\) any immersed submanifold of codimension \(1\) with the induced metric. \(\Sigma\) will be called hypersurface if \(M\).

Then the tangen bundle \(TM\) can be decomposed in the following sum

\begin{equation} \label{eq:sum_TBundle} TM = \left(T\Sigma\right)^{\top} \oplus \left(T\Sigma\right)^{\bot}. \end{equation}

Let \(D\) the Levi-Civita connection for the ambient manifold \(M\), then it is a exercise to prove that, for any \(X,Y\in T\Sigma\) the projection \(\left(D_{X}Y\right)^{\top}\) is the Levi-Civita connection of on \(\Sigma\), denoted by \(\nabla\).

Let \(\xi\) a normal vector to \(\Sigma\). The second fundamental form \(h\) is the normal projection of \(D\) for every \(X,Y\in T\Sigma\)

\begin{equation} h(X,Y) = g(D_XY,\xi). \end{equation}

The Gauss formula for \(X,Y\in T\Sigma\) reads

\begin{equation} \label{eq:Gauss_Formula} D_{X}Y = \nabla_{X}Y + h(X,Y)\xi. \end{equation}

Note that from \(g(Y,\xi)=0\) and the compatibility with the metric we have

\begin{equation} g(D_{X}Y,\xi) = g(Y, -D_{X}\xi). \end{equation}

For a normal vector \(\xi\) we define the shape operator \(A_{\xi}:T\Sigma\to T\Sigma\) as

\begin{equation} \label{eq:Shape_Operator} A_{\xi}(X) = \left(-D_{X}\xi\right)^{\top}. \end{equation}

Let \(\{E_1,...,E_n,\xi\}\) be a basis for \(TM\) such that \(\{E_i\}\) span \(T\Sigma\) and \(\xi\) is unit normal to \(\Sigma\). Then the shape operator in basis elements can be written as

\begin{equation} A_{\xi}(E_j) = A_j^i E_i,\qquad i,j=1,2,...,n. \end{equation}

Since there is only one normal direction the second fundamental \(h(X,Y)=g(h(X,Y),\xi)\xi\) can be identify with a real number \(g(h(X,Y),\xi)\), and from the definition of the shape operator we can write in coordinates

\begin{equation} h_{kj} =g(h(E_k,E_j),\xi) = g(E_k,A_{\xi}(E_j)) = g(E_k,A_j^iE_i) = g_{ki}A^i_j, \end{equation}

equivalently

\begin{equation} A^i_j = g^{ik}h_{kj}. \end{equation}

The principal curvatures of the hypersurface \(\Sigma\) are the eigenvalues of the matrix \((A^i_j)\).

Example: Hypersurfaces as graphs in Euclidean space.

Consider that \(\Sigma\subset\mathbb{R}^{n+1}\) is parametrised as the graph of a smooth function \(f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}\). Then at any point \(p\in\Sigma\) there is a neighbourhood \(U\in\mathbb{R}^n\) such that every \(y\in\Sigma\cap f(U)\) can be written as \(y = (x,f(x))\) for some \(x=(x^1,...,x^n)\in U\).

In this coordinates, the basis for the tangent space \(T_p\Sigma\) is given by

\begin{equation} y_i = \frac{\partial y}{\partial x^i} = (e_i,f_i), \end{equation}

where \(\{e_i\}\) is the standard basis of \(\mathbb{R}^n\). Let \(Df = (f_1,...,f_n)\) the usual gradient vector of \(f\). Then a unit normal vector field in \(\Sigma\) is given by

\begin{equation} \hat{n} = \frac{(-D f,1)}{\sqrt{1+|D f|^2}}. \end{equation}

To compute the second fundamental form \(h_{ij}=g(D_jy_i,n)\) we observe that

\begin{equation} D_jy_i = \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^j\partial x^i} = (0,f_{ij}), \end{equation}

then its normal projection is

\begin{equation} h_{ij} = \langle D_j y_i,\hat{n}\rangle = \frac{ f_{ij}}{\sqrt{1 + |D f|^2}}. \end{equation}

Since we are using the standar metric of the Euclidean space, then the induce metric on \(\Sigma\) is given by

\begin{equation} g_{ij} = \langle y_i , y_j \rangle = \delta_{ij} + f_if_j \end{equation}

The inverse of the metric is then

\begin{equation} g^{ij} = \delta_{ij} - \frac{f_if_j}{1+|D f|^2}, \end{equation}

then the shape operator is given by

\begin{equation} \label{eq:Shape_Of_Graph} A^i_j = g^{ik}h_{kj} = \sum_k{\left(\delta_{ik} - \frac{f_if_k}{1+|D f|^2}\right) \left(\frac{f_{kj}}{\sqrt{1 + |D f|^2}}\right)} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x^j}\left(\frac{ f_i}{\sqrt{1+|D f|^2}}\right). \end{equation}
