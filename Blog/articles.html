Geometry of hypersurfaces.

Posted by: Daniel B.
[2019-05-16 Thu]

When we are trying to understand the geometry of a space, we usually wonder what are the properties whithin the space. Gauss's Theorema Egregium, for example, shows how for surfaces in the three dimensional space the Gaussian curvature is an intrinsic invariant. On the other hand, we have also properties that change depending on how one represents the geometric objects.

Let \((M^{n+1},g)\) be a Riemannian manifold and \(\Sigma^n\subset M^{n+1}\) any immersed submanifold of codimension \(1\) with the induced metric. \(\Sigma\) will be called hypersurface if \(M\).

Then the tangen bundle \(TM\) can be decomposed in the following sum

\begin{equation} \label{eq:sum_TBundle} TM = \left(T\Sigma\right)^{\top} \oplus \left(T\Sigma\right)^{\bot}. \end{equation}

Let \(D\) the Levi-Civita connection for the ambient manifold \(M\), then it is a exercise to prove that, for any \(X,Y\in T\Sigma\) the projection \(\left(D_{X}Y\right)^{\top}\) is the Levi-Civita connection of on \(\Sigma\), denoted by \(\nabla\).

Let \(\xi\) a normal vector to \(\Sigma\). The second fundamental form \(h\) is the normal projection of \(D\) for every \(X,Y\in T\Sigma\)

\begin{equation} h(X,Y) = g(D_XY,\xi). \end{equation}

The Gauss formula for \(X,Y\in T\Sigma\) reads

\begin{equation} \label{eq:Gauss_Formula} D_{X}Y = \nabla_{X}Y + h(X,Y)\xi. \end{equation}

Note that from \(g(Y,\xi)=0\) and the compatibility with the metric we have

\begin{equation} g(D_{X}Y,\xi) = g(Y, -D_{X}\xi). \end{equation}

For a normal vector \(\xi\) we define the shape operator \(A_{\xi}:T\Sigma\to T\Sigma\) as

\begin{equation} \label{eq:Shape_Operator} A_{\xi}(X) = \left(-D_{X}\xi\right)^{\top}. \end{equation}

Let \(\{E_1,...,E_n,\xi\}\) be a basis for \(TM\) such that \(\{E_i\}\) span \(T\Sigma\) and \(\xi\) is unit normal to \(\Sigma\). Then the shape operator in basis elements can be written as

\begin{equation} A_{\xi}(E_j) = A_j^i E_i,\qquad i,j=1,2,...,n. \end{equation}

Since there is only one normal direction the second fundamental \(h(X,Y)=g(h(X,Y),\xi)\xi\) can be identify with a real number \(g(h(X,Y),\xi)\), and from the definition of the shape operator \eqref{eq:Shape_Operator} on the basis elements we have

\begin{equation} h_{kj} =g(h(E_k,E_j),\xi) = g(E_k,A_{\xi}(E_j)) = g(E_k,A_j^iE_i) = g_{ki}A^i_j, \end{equation}

That is to say

\begin{equation} A^i_j = g^{ik}h_{kj}. \end{equation}

The principal curvatures of the hypersurface \(\Sigma\) are the eigenvalues of the matrix \((A^i_j)\).

Example: Hypersurfaces as graphs in Euclidean space.

Consider that \(\Sigma\subset\mathbb{R}^{n+1}\) is parametrised as the graph of a smooth function \(f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}\). Then at any point \(p\in\Sigma\) there is a neighbourhood \(U\in\mathbb{R}^n\) such that every \(y\in\Sigma\cap f(U)\) can be written as \(y = (x,f(x))\) for some \(x=(x^1,...,x^n)\in U\).

In this coordinates, the basis for the tangent space \(T_p\Sigma\) is given by

\begin{equation} y_i = \frac{\partial y}{\partial x^i} = (e_i,f_i), \end{equation}

where \(\{e_i\}\) is the standard basis of \(\mathbb{R}^n\). Let \(Df = (f_1,...,f_n)\) the usual gradient vector of \(f\). Then a unit normal vector field in \(\Sigma\) is given by

\begin{equation} \hat{n} = \frac{(-D f,1)}{\sqrt{1+|D f|^2}}. \end{equation}

To compute the second fundamental form \(h_{ij}=g(D_jy_i,n)\) we observe that

\begin{equation} D_jy_i = \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^j\partial x^i} = (0,f_{ij}), \end{equation}

then its normal projection is

\begin{equation} h_{ij} = \langle D_j y_i,\hat{n}\rangle = \frac{ f_{ij}}{\sqrt{1 + |D f|^2}}. \end{equation}

Since we are using the standar metric of the Euclidean space, then the induce metric on \(\Sigma\) is given by

\begin{equation} g_{ij} = \langle y_i , y_j \rangle = \delta_{ij} + f_if_j \end{equation}

The inverse of the metric is then

\begin{equation} g^{ij} = \delta_{ij} - \frac{f_if_j}{1+|D f|^2}, \end{equation}

then the shape operator is given by

\begin{equation} \label{eq:Shape_Of_Graph} A^i_j = g^{ik}h_{kj} = \sum_k{\left(\delta_{ik} - \frac{f_if_k}{1+|D f|^2}\right) \left(\frac{f_{kj}}{\sqrt{1 + |D f|^2}}\right)} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x^j}\left(\frac{ f_i}{\sqrt{1+|D f|^2}}\right). \end{equation}

Existence of Geodesics With a Heat Flow.

Posted by: Daniel B.
[2019-05-14 Tue]

If we want to obtain a closed geodesic in a compact Riemannian manifold all we have to do is get one loop and wait. Yes just wait long enough and, surprisingly, our loop will transform into a geodesic one. Moreover, take a smooth close loop in a compact Riemannian manifold, of course this belongs to a homotopy class. Suppose now that we are able to measure the energy of our loop with some sort of thermometer, and that changes of this energy may produce some elongation or contraction of the loop, measured by its length. Then we wait for a reasonable long period of time for our loop to cool down and.. we end up with a geodesic of the same homotopy class!

Well, unfortunately not quite. The problem of passing from \(t_n\) to \(t\) (see below) in the convergence is something that needs to be treated carefully.

The curve \(\gamma : (a,b) \subset \mathbb{R} \to M\) is geodesic if and only if in local coordinates it satisfies

\begin{equation} \ddot{\gamma}^k(t) + \dot{\gamma}^i(t)\dot{\gamma}^j)(t)\Gamma_{ij}^{k}(\gamma(t)) = 0. \end{equation}

Before we continue just a word of warning: note that for any compact Riemannian manifold, and two points in it, there are always more than one geodesic joining them, a quick example of this is the sphere with the North and South pole. The uniqueness of the shortest geodesic is guaranteed when the two points are sufficiently close. The method we will describe, produces a geodesic but It may not necessarily be the shortest one.

Theorem Let \(M\) be a compact Riemannian manifold. Then every homotopy class of closed curves in \(M\) contains a geodesic.

To apply the heat flow approach, we consider the following family of curves:

\begin{equation} u : S^1 \times [0,\infty) \to M, \end{equation}

where \(S^1\) is the unite circle, and the parabolic equation

\begin{equation} \begin{split} u_{t} &= u^k_{ss} + u^i_{s}u^j_{s}\Gamma_{ij}^k & s\in S^1, t\geq 0,\\ u(s,0)& = \gamma(s) & s\in S^1. \end{split} \end{equation}

The Heat Flow Method

Now we write the method to obtain the geodesic as outline by J. Jost in his Riemannian Geometry and Geometric Analysis.

  1. Short time existence. There exist \(T_0>0\) such that the initial value problem above has a solution \(u^k_{t} = u^k_{ss} + u^i_{s}u^j_{s}\Gamma_{ij}^k\) in \(S^1\times [0,T_0)\) and \(u(s,0)=\gamma(s)\). Then the maximal (time) interval of existence of a solution is nonempty and open.
  2. A priori estimates of spacial derivatives. Let \(u(s,t)\) be a solution. Get an a priori estimate of the spacial derivative \(|u_s| \leq C\), where \(C\) independently of \(t\). Then we can think of the equation \(u_t - u_{ss} =f\), where \(f\) is a bounded function. Then regularity theory for linear parabolic equations is used to control higher order derivatives which are time independent.
  3. Convergence as \(t\) goes to \(\infty\). This a priori estimate implies the following: If a solution exists in \([0,T_{0})\), and for \(t\to T_{0}\) the solution \(u(s,t)\) will converge to a smooth curve \(u(s,T_{0})\). Then this curve can be taken as the new initial value and continue the solution for times bigger than \(T_{0}\). Then the maximal interval of existence of a solution is also a closed set. This at the end implies that a solution exists for all times \(t>0\).
  4. The Energy functional is decreasing. Note that the energy \(E(u(s,t))\) is decreasing in \(t\) and since \(0 \leq E(u(s,t))\) for all \(t\), it is possible to find a sequence \(\{t_n\}\) such that \(u(s,t) \to \tilde{\gamma}(s)\), \(t\to \infty\), and \(\tilde{\gamma}\) is such that \(\tilde{\gamma}^k_{ss} + \tilde{\gamma}^i_{s}\tilde{\gamma}^j_{s}\Gamma_{ij}^k = 0\).
  5. Convexity. If convexity is not fulfilled, then things that may look like wiggles may happen to have less energy than the geodesic. We also have to be careful with the curvature of our manifold. When (non-negative) curvature condition is fulfilled and convexity is checked, then the convergence to the geodesic can be taken from \(t\to\infty\), and is not restricted to the sequence \(t_n\).

One of the things I learnt from this exercise is to be careful when using normal coordinates and integrating by parts. We will go through the computations of \(E\), \(E_t\) and \(E_{tt}\).

The Energy functional of a curve \(\alpha\) is given by \(E(u):= \frac{1}{2}\int_{S^1} \|\dot{\alpha}(t)\|^2dt\), equivallently in coordinates:

\begin{equation} E(u(\cdot,t)) = \frac{1}{2}\int_{S^1} g_{ij}\alpha^i_s\alpha^j_s ds \end{equation}
